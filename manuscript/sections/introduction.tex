Large production clusters use elastic resource control to balance service quality and resource efficiency. Many stacks still rely on reactive threshold rules because they are simple and explainable \citep{lorido2014review,Burns2016BorgKubernetes,Bernstein2014ContainersCloud}, but such rules often scale out after demand has already surged and scale in only after long idle periods. This creates a recurring trade-off between service-level violations and persistent over-provisioning.

Predictive autoscaling addresses this delay, but two gaps remain. Many studies evaluate forecasting and control separately, so it is unclear how lower prediction error changes policy trade-offs. Many strong cluster-management results also depend on private traces or platform-specific deployments, limiting reproducibility and comparability.

Prior work on host-load prediction \citep{Dinda2000HostLoad}, container autoscaling \citep{Casalicchio2019ContainerAutoscaling}, and uncertainty-aware workload forecasting on Alibaba and Google traces \citep{Rossi2025UncertaintyAwareForecasting} already links forecasting, autoscaling, and resource management. The remaining need is a joint evaluation of prediction quality, control behavior, and validity limits.

This paper asks a narrower question: can public cluster traces support a reproducible predictive capacity-control evaluation that shows when decision trade-offs improve relative to reactive rules? The study uses Alibaba \texttt{cluster-trace-v2018}, whose documentation reports about 4000 machines over 8 days \citep{alibabaclusterdata2026,alibabav2018trace}. Prior Alibaba analyses report diverse workloads and nontrivial resource-efficiency structure \citep{Jiang2022Characterizing,guo2019limits,luo2021characterizing}, making the trace suitable for this evaluation.

The pipeline routes multi-horizon forecasts into an autoscaling simulator with headroom, cooldown, and safety-margin constraints. This keeps the controller interpretable while allowing the forecasting backbone to be replaced without changing the evaluation route.

The evidence spans an aggregate series, a 12-machine high-coverage cohort, a cross-machine transfer benchmark, and a rule-based service cohort extracted from official \texttt{container\_meta} and a full mirrored pass over \texttt{container\_usage}. This structure tests whether aggregate forecasting and control patterns survive at machine and application-level units.

The implementation remains conservative. \texttt{UA-MSTCN-Lite} is a lightweight uncertainty-aware surrogate rather than the final deep UA-MSTCN architecture, and the service cohort is a broad rule-based benchmark rather than a full service census. The contribution is therefore evaluative: a reproducible public-trace benchmark and boundary-mapping study, not a claim that the current controller or surrogate is uniformly better.

This paper makes four evaluation-oriented contributions:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item It frames predictive cluster capacity control as a reproducible public-trace evaluation problem.
  \item It provides a pipeline from Alibaba trace acquisition to aggregate-, machine-, and service-level preprocessing, forecasting, and policy simulation.
  \item It reports one consistent evidence surface across aggregate forecasting, machine-level benchmarking, cross-machine transfer, and service-level heterogeneity under one consistent protocol.
  \item It maps the boundary of the current predictive route: linear baselines remain strong for point prediction, quantiles remain useful for control, and broad service evidence does not validate the shared global predictive policy as a service-level improvement route.
\end{enumerate}

All preprocessing scripts, model training code, and the predictive-control simulator are publicly released at \url{https://github.com/Jinchun-Liu/paper-workbench} to guarantee exact reproduction of every reported number.
